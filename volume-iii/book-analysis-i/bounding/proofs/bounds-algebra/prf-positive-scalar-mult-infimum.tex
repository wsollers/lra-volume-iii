\begin{theorem*}[Positive Scalar Multiplication of the Infimum]
Let $A\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ be nonempty and bounded below, and let $k>0$.
Then
\[
  \inf(kA)=k\inf A,
  \qquad
  kA=\{ka:a\in A\}.
\]
\end{theorem*}

\begin{proof}[Professional Standard Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Let \(\ell=\inf A\). Since \(\ell\) is a lower bound of \(A\), we have
\(\ell\le a\) for every \(a\in A\). Because \(k>0\), multiplication by \(k\)
preserves order, so
\[
  k\ell\le ka
\]
for every \(a\in A\). Thus \(k\ell\) is a lower bound of \(kA\).

Let \(b\) be any lower bound of \(kA\). Then for every \(a\in A\),
\[
  b\le ka.
\]
Dividing by \(k>0\) preserves order, so
\[
  \frac{b}{k}\le a
\]
for every \(a\in A\). Hence \(b/k\) is a lower bound of \(A\). Since
\(\ell=\inf A\), the greatest-lower-bound property gives \(b/k\le \ell\).
Multiplying by \(k>0\) yields \(b\le k\ell\).

Therefore every lower bound of \(kA\) lies below \(k\ell\), and \(k\ell\) is
itself a lower bound of \(kA\). Hence \(k\ell=\inf(kA)\), so
\[
  \inf(kA)=k\inf A.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Detailed Learning Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Let
\[
  \ell=\inf A.
\]
We will prove that \(k\ell\) is the infimum of
\[
  kA=\{ka:a\in A\}.
\]
To prove that a number is an infimum, we must prove two things: it is a lower
bound, and it is the greatest among all lower bounds.

First we prove the lower-bound part. Take any element of \(kA\). By the
definition of \(kA\), it has the form \(ka\) for some \(a\in A\). Since
\(\ell=\inf A\), \(\ell\) is a lower bound of \(A\), so
\[
  \ell\le a.
\]
Because \(k>0\), multiplying both sides by \(k\) preserves the inequality:
\[
  k\ell\le ka.
\]
Thus \(k\ell\) is less than or equal to every element of \(kA\), so \(k\ell\)
is a lower bound of \(kA\).

Next we prove greatestness. Let \(b\) be any lower bound of \(kA\). This means
that \(b\) lies below every element \(ka\) with \(a\in A\):
\[
  b\le ka
  \qquad(a\in A).
\]
Since \(k>0\), dividing by \(k\) preserves order, giving
\[
  \frac{b}{k}\le a
  \qquad(a\in A).
\]
So \(b/k\) is a lower bound of \(A\). Because \(\ell=\inf A\), \(\ell\) is the
greatest lower bound of \(A\), which means every lower bound of \(A\) is less
than or equal to \(\ell\). Applying this to the lower bound \(b/k\), we obtain
\[
  \frac{b}{k}\le \ell.
\]
Multiplying again by the positive number \(k\), we get
\[
  b\le k\ell.
\]

We have shown that every lower bound \(b\) of \(kA\) lies below \(k\ell\).
Together with the fact that \(k\ell\) is itself a lower bound of \(kA\), this
proves that \(k\ell\) is the greatest lower bound of \(kA\). Therefore
\[
  \inf(kA)=k\ell=k\inf A.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark*}[Proof structure]
Positive multiplication preserves the order direction. The lower-bound part
moves \(\ell\le a\) to \(k\ell\le ka\). The greatestness part takes an
arbitrary lower bound \(b\) of \(kA\), divides by \(k>0\) to obtain the lower
bound \(b/k\) of \(A\), then scales the comparison \(b/k\le\ell\) back to
\(b\le k\ell\).
\end{remark*}
