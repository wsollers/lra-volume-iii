\begin{theorem*}[Translation Invariance of the Infimum]
Let $A\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ be nonempty and bounded below, and let
$c\in\mathbb{R}$. Then
\[
  \inf(A+c)=\inf A+c,
  \qquad
  A+c=\{a+c:a\in A\}.
\]
\end{theorem*}

\begin{proof}[Professional Standard Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Let \(\ell=\inf A\). Since \(A\) is nonempty, \(A+c\) is nonempty. For every
\(a\in A\), the lower-bound property of \(\ell\) gives \(\ell\le a\), and
therefore
\[
  \ell+c\le a+c.
\]
Thus \(\ell+c\) is a lower bound of \(A+c\).

Now let \(b\) be any lower bound of \(A+c\). Then for every \(a\in A\),
\[
  b\le a+c,
\]
so \(b-c\le a\). Hence \(b-c\) is a lower bound of \(A\). Since
\(\ell=\inf A\), the greatest-lower-bound property gives \(b-c\le \ell\), and
therefore
\[
  b\le \ell+c.
\]
So every lower bound of \(A+c\) lies below \(\ell+c\). Since \(\ell+c\) is
itself a lower bound of \(A+c\), it is the infimum of \(A+c\). Hence
\[
  \inf(A+c)=\ell+c=\inf A+c.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Detailed Learning Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Let
\[
  \ell=\inf A.
\]
We will prove that \(\ell+c\) is the infimum of the translated set
\[
  A+c=\{a+c:a\in A\}.
\]
To prove that a number is an infimum, we must prove two things: it is a lower
bound, and it is the greatest among all lower bounds.

First we prove the lower-bound part. Take any element of \(A+c\). By the
definition of \(A+c\), it has the form \(a+c\) for some \(a\in A\). Since
\(\ell=\inf A\), \(\ell\) is a lower bound of \(A\), so
\[
  \ell\le a.
\]
Adding \(c\) to both sides preserves the order, so
\[
  \ell+c\le a+c.
\]
Therefore \(\ell+c\) is less than or equal to every element of \(A+c\). Thus
\(\ell+c\) is a lower bound of \(A+c\).

Next we prove greatestness. Let \(b\) be any lower bound of \(A+c\). This
means that \(b\) lies below every translated element: for every \(a\in A\),
\[
  b\le a+c.
\]
Subtracting \(c\) from both sides gives
\[
  b-c\le a
\]
for every \(a\in A\). Hence \(b-c\) is a lower bound of the original set
\(A\). Because \(\ell=\inf A\), \(\ell\) is greater than or equal to every
lower bound of \(A\). Applying this to the lower bound \(b-c\), we get
\[
  b-c\le \ell.
\]
Adding \(c\) to both sides gives
\[
  b\le \ell+c.
\]

We have shown that every lower bound \(b\) of \(A+c\) lies below \(\ell+c\).
Together with the fact that \(\ell+c\) is itself a lower bound of \(A+c\),
this proves that \(\ell+c\) is the greatest lower bound of \(A+c\). Therefore
\[
  \inf(A+c)=\ell+c=\inf A+c.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark*}[Proof structure]
Translate both pieces of the infimum definition. The lower-bound part moves
from \(\ell\le a\) to \(\ell+c\le a+c\). The greatestness part tests an
arbitrary lower bound \(b\) of \(A+c\), translates it back to the lower bound
\(b-c\) of \(A\), and then translates the comparison \(b-c\le\ell\) forward
again.
\end{remark*}
