\begin{theorem*}[Supremum of a Dilation]
Let $A\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ be nonempty and bounded, and let $\lambda\in\mathbb{R}$. Then
\[
  \sup(\lambda A)=\max\{\lambda\sup A,\lambda\inf A\},
  \qquad
  \lambda A=\{\lambda a:a\in A\}.
\]
\end{theorem*}

\begin{proof}[Professional Standard Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Let $s=\sup A$ and $i=\inf A$.

If $\lambda=0$, then $\lambda A=\{0\}$ because $A$ is nonempty. Hence
\[
  \sup(\lambda A)=0=\max\{0s,0i\}.
\]

If $\lambda>0$, the positive scalar multiplication theorem gives
\[
  \sup(\lambda A)=\lambda\sup A=\lambda s.
\]
Since $i\le s$ and $\lambda>0$, we have $\lambda i\le \lambda s$, so
\[
  \max\{\lambda s,\lambda i\}=\lambda s=\sup(\lambda A).
\]

If $\lambda<0$, the negative scalar multiplication theorem gives
\[
  \sup(\lambda A)=\lambda\inf A=\lambda i.
\]
Since $i\le s$ and multiplication by the negative scalar $\lambda$ reverses
order, $\lambda s\le \lambda i$. Therefore
\[
  \max\{\lambda s,\lambda i\}=\lambda i=\sup(\lambda A).
\]

The three cases cover every real value of $\lambda$, so
\[
  \sup(\lambda A)=\max\{\lambda\sup A,\lambda\inf A\}.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Detailed Learning Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Write
\[
  s=\sup A
  \qquad\text{and}\qquad
  i=\inf A.
\]
Because $A$ is nonempty and bounded, both $s$ and $i$ exist, and
$i\le s$.

We split according to the sign of $\lambda$.

First suppose $\lambda=0$. Then every element of $\lambda A$ has the form
$0a=0$, and since $A$ is nonempty the image is exactly $\{0\}$. Thus
\[
  \sup(\lambda A)=\sup\{0\}=0.
\]
At the same time,
\[
  \max\{\lambda s,\lambda i\}=\max\{0,0\}=0,
\]
so the formula holds in the zero case.

Now suppose $\lambda>0$. Positive scaling preserves order, and the already
proved positive-scalar theorem says that the supremum scales by the same
positive factor:
\[
  \sup(\lambda A)=\lambda s.
\]
Since $i\le s$, multiplying by the positive number $\lambda$ preserves the
inequality:
\[
  \lambda i\le \lambda s.
\]
Therefore the larger of the two candidate endpoint values is $\lambda s$:
\[
  \max\{\lambda s,\lambda i\}=\lambda s.
\]
Combining this with $\sup(\lambda A)=\lambda s$ gives the desired equality
when $\lambda>0$.

Finally suppose $\lambda<0$. Negative scaling reverses order, and the already
proved negative-scalar theorem says that the supremum of the reflected and
scaled set comes from the infimum of the original set:
\[
  \sup(\lambda A)=\lambda i.
\]
Again $i\le s$, but now multiplying by the negative number $\lambda$ reverses
the inequality:
\[
  \lambda s\le \lambda i.
\]
Thus the larger endpoint value is $\lambda i$:
\[
  \max\{\lambda s,\lambda i\}=\lambda i.
\]
Together with $\sup(\lambda A)=\lambda i$, this proves the formula when
$\lambda<0$.

The cases $\lambda=0$, $\lambda>0$, and $\lambda<0$ exhaust all real scalars.
Hence
\[
  \sup(\lambda A)=\max\{\lambda\sup A,\lambda\inf A\}.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark*}[Proof structure]
Separate the scalar into zero, positive, and negative cases. The zero case
collapses the image to $\{0\}$; the positive case uses the positive-scalar
supremum theorem; the negative case uses the negative-scalar supremum theorem.
The maximum records which endpoint becomes the upper endpoint after the
scaling.
\end{remark*}
