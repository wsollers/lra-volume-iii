\begin{theorem*}[Inverse Function Theorem, One Variable]
Let \(I\subseteq\mathbb{R}\) be open, let \(f:I\to\mathbb{R}\) be \(C^1\), and suppose \(f'(c)\neq 0\). Then \(f\) has a \(C^1\) local inverse near \(c\), and the inverse satisfies \(g'(y)=1/f'(g(y))\).
\end{theorem*}

\begin{proof}[Professional Standard Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Since \(f'\) is continuous and \(f'(c)\neq 0\), sign preservation gives an open interval \(U\ni c\) on which \(f'\) has constant nonzero sign. Replacing \(U\) by a smaller interval if needed, assume \(f'>0\) on \(U\); the case \(f'<0\) is identical with inequalities reversed. The monotonicity theorem implies that \(f\) is strictly increasing on \(U\), hence injective. Let \(V=f(U)\). Since \(f\) is continuous and strictly monotone on an interval, \(V\) is an open interval and \(f|_U:U\to V\) has a continuous inverse \(g:V\to U\).

Fix \(y_0\in V\), and set \(x_0=g(y_0)\). By the Carath\'{e}odory characterization, there is a function \(\varphi\), continuous at \(x_0\), such that
\[
  f(x)-f(x_0)=(x-x_0)\varphi(x),
  \qquad
  \varphi(x_0)=f'(x_0)\neq 0.
\]
Substituting \(x=g(y)\) gives
\[
  y-y_0=(g(y)-g(y_0))\varphi(g(y)).
\]
Because \(g\) and \(\varphi\) are continuous and \(\varphi(g(y_0))\neq 0\), the reciprocal \(1/\varphi(g(y))\) is continuous near \(y_0\). Hence
\[
  g(y)-g(y_0)=\frac{1}{\varphi(g(y))}(y-y_0),
\]
which is a Carath\'{e}odory factorization for \(g\) at \(y_0\). Therefore \(g\) is differentiable at \(y_0\) and
\[
  g'(y_0)=\frac{1}{\varphi(g(y_0))}=\frac{1}{f'(g(y_0))}.
\]
Since \(y_0\) was arbitrary and \(1/(f'\circ g)\) is continuous on \(V\), \(g\in C^1(V)\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Detailed Learning Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
The proof has two stages. First, continuity of \(f'\) turns the pointwise condition \(f'(c)\neq 0\) into a neighbourhood condition: \(f'\) keeps one sign near \(c\). The sign of the derivative forces strict monotonicity on that neighbourhood, and strict monotonicity gives the local inverse.

Second, the derivative of the inverse is computed without dividing by \(f(x)-f(x_0)\). Carath\'{e}odory writes the increment of \(f\) as \((x-x_0)\varphi(x)\). After substituting \(x=g(y)\), the same identity can be solved for the increment of \(g\), producing a continuous slope factor for \(g\). Its value at \(y_0\) is the reciprocal of \(f'(x_0)\).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark*}[Proof structure]
Use sign preservation to get monotonicity and local invertibility, then use Carath\'{e}odory factorization to prove differentiability of the inverse.
\end{remark*}
