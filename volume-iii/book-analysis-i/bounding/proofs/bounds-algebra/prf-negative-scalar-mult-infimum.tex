\begin{theorem*}[Negative Scalar Multiplication of the Infimum]
Let $A\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ be nonempty and bounded above, and let $k<0$.
Then
\[
  \inf(kA)=k\sup A,
  \qquad
  kA=\{ka:a\in A\}.
\]
\end{theorem*}

\begin{proof}[Professional Standard Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Let \(s=\sup A\). Since \(s\) is an upper bound of \(A\), we have \(a\le s\)
for every \(a\in A\). Because \(k<0\), multiplication by \(k\) reverses order,
so
\[
  ks\le ka
\]
for every \(a\in A\). Thus \(ks\) is a lower bound of \(kA\).

Let \(b\) be any lower bound of \(kA\). Then for every \(a\in A\),
\[
  b\le ka.
\]
Dividing by \(k<0\) reverses order, so
\[
  \frac{b}{k}\ge a
\]
for every \(a\in A\). Hence \(b/k\) is an upper bound of \(A\). Since
\(s=\sup A\), the least-upper-bound property gives \(s\le b/k\). Multiplying
by \(k<0\) reverses order and yields \(ks\ge b\), equivalently \(b\le ks\).

Therefore every lower bound of \(kA\) lies below \(ks\), and \(ks\) is itself
a lower bound of \(kA\). Hence \(ks=\inf(kA)\), so
\[
  \inf(kA)=k\sup A.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Detailed Learning Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Let
\[
  s=\sup A.
\]
We will prove that \(ks\) is the infimum of
\[
  kA=\{ka:a\in A\}.
\]
Since \(k<0\), multiplying an inequality by \(k\) reverses its direction.

First we prove that \(ks\) is a lower bound of \(kA\). Take any element of
\(kA\). By the definition of \(kA\), it has the form \(ka\) for some
\(a\in A\). Since \(s=\sup A\), \(s\) is an upper bound of \(A\), so
\[
  a\le s.
\]
Multiplying by the negative scalar \(k\) reverses the inequality:
\[
  ka\ge ks.
\]
Equivalently, \(ks\le ka\). Thus \(ks\) is less than or equal to every element
of \(kA\), so \(ks\) is a lower bound of \(kA\).

Next we prove that this lower bound is the greatest one. Let \(b\) be any
lower bound of \(kA\). This means that \(b\) lies below every element \(ka\)
with \(a\in A\):
\[
  b\le ka
  \qquad(a\in A).
\]
Dividing by the negative number \(k\) reverses order, giving
\[
  \frac{b}{k}\ge a
  \qquad(a\in A).
\]
So \(b/k\) is an upper bound of \(A\). Since \(s=\sup A\), \(s\) is less than
or equal to every upper bound of \(A\). Applying this to the upper bound
\(b/k\), we obtain
\[
  s\le \frac{b}{k}.
\]
Multiplying by \(k<0\) reverses the inequality once more:
\[
  ks\ge b.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
  b\le ks.
\]

We have shown that every lower bound \(b\) of \(kA\) lies below \(ks\).
Together with the fact that \(ks\) is itself a lower bound of \(kA\), this
proves that \(ks\) is the greatest lower bound of \(kA\). Therefore
\[
  \inf(kA)=ks=k\sup A.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark*}[Proof structure]
Negative multiplication reverses order. The upper-bound fact \(a\le s\) for
\(s=\sup A\) becomes the lower-bound fact \(ks\le ka\). To prove greatestness,
translate an arbitrary lower bound \(b\) of \(kA\) back by dividing by
\(k<0\); the reversed inequality makes \(b/k\) an upper bound of \(A\). The
comparison \(s\le b/k\) then reverses back to \(b\le ks\).
\end{remark*}
