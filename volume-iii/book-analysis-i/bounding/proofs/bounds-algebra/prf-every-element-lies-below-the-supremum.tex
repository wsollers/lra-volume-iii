\begin{proposition*}[Every Element Lies Below the Supremum]
If $s = \sup A$, then for every $x \in A$, $x \le s$.
\end{proposition*}

\begin{proof}[Professional Standard Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Since \(s=\sup A\), the definition of supremum says, in particular, that
\(s\) is an upper bound of \(A\). Hence every element of \(A\) lies below
\(s\): for every \(x\in A\),
\[
x\le s.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Detailed Learning Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Let \(x\in A\) be arbitrary. We need to prove that \(x\le s\).

The hypothesis \(s=\sup A\) means that \(s\) is the least upper bound of
\(A\). In particular, before using the ``least'' part, we know that \(s\) is
an upper bound of \(A\). By the definition of upper bound, this means that
every element of \(A\) is less than or equal to \(s\). Since \(x\in A\), it
follows that
\[
x\le s.
\]

Because \(x\in A\) was arbitrary, \(x\le s\) for every \(x\in A\).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark*}[Proof structure]
This proof only unpacks the definition of supremum. The conclusion uses the
upper-bound part of \(s=\sup A\); the minimality of \(s\) among upper bounds is
not needed.
\end{remark*}
