\begin{theorem*}[Translation Invariance of the Supremum]
Let $A\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ be nonempty and bounded above, and let $c\in\mathbb{R}$. Then
\[
  \sup(A+c)=\sup A+c,
  \qquad
  A+c=\{a+c:a\in A\}.
\]
\end{theorem*}

\begin{proof}[Professional Standard Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Let \(s=\sup A\). Since \(A\) is nonempty, \(A+c\) is nonempty. For every
\(a\in A\), the upper-bound property of \(s\) gives \(a\le s\), and therefore
\[
  a+c\le s+c.
\]
Thus \(s+c\) is an upper bound of \(A+c\).

Now let \(v\) be any upper bound of \(A+c\). Then for every \(a\in A\),
\[
  a+c\le v,
\]
so \(a\le v-c\). Hence \(v-c\) is an upper bound of \(A\). Since \(s=\sup A\),
the least-upper-bound property gives \(s\le v-c\), and therefore
\[
  s+c\le v.
\]
So \(s+c\) is less than or equal to every upper bound of \(A+c\). Since it is
also an upper bound of \(A+c\), it is the supremum of \(A+c\). Hence
\[
  \sup(A+c)=s+c=\sup A+c.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Detailed Learning Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Let
\[
  s=\sup A.
\]
We will prove that \(s+c\) is the supremum of the translated set
\[
  A+c=\{a+c:a\in A\}.
\]
To prove that a number is a supremum, we must prove two things: it is an upper
bound, and it is the least among all upper bounds.

First we prove the upper-bound part. Take any element of \(A+c\). By the
definition of \(A+c\), it has the form \(a+c\) for some \(a\in A\). Since
\(s=\sup A\), \(s\) is an upper bound of \(A\), so
\[
  a\le s.
\]
Adding \(c\) to both sides preserves the order, so
\[
  a+c\le s+c.
\]
Therefore every element of \(A+c\) is less than or equal to \(s+c\). Thus
\(s+c\) is an upper bound of \(A+c\).

Next we prove leastness. Let \(v\) be any upper bound of \(A+c\). This means
that every translated element lies below \(v\): for every \(a\in A\),
\[
  a+c\le v.
\]
Subtracting \(c\) from both sides gives
\[
  a\le v-c
\]
for every \(a\in A\). Hence \(v-c\) is an upper bound of the original set
\(A\). Because \(s=\sup A\), \(s\) is less than or equal to every upper bound
of \(A\). Applying this to the upper bound \(v-c\), we get
\[
  s\le v-c.
\]
Adding \(c\) to both sides gives
\[
  s+c\le v.
\]

We have shown that every upper bound \(v\) of \(A+c\) lies above \(s+c\).
Together with the fact that \(s+c\) is itself an upper bound of \(A+c\), this
proves that \(s+c\) is the least upper bound of \(A+c\). Therefore
\[
  \sup(A+c)=s+c=\sup A+c.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark*}[Proof structure]
Translate both pieces of the supremum definition. The upper-bound part moves
from \(a\le s\) to \(a+c\le s+c\). The leastness part tests an arbitrary upper
bound \(v\) of \(A+c\), translates it back to the upper bound \(v-c\) of
\(A\), and then translates the comparison \(s\le v-c\) forward again.
\end{remark*}
