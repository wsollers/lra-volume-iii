\section*{Difference Formulas for Special Functions}

The table below collects the difference formulas for the principal
special functions of discrete calculus.

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=gray!6, colframe=gray!40, arc=2pt,
  left=6pt, right=6pt, top=4pt, bottom=4pt,
  title={\small\textbf{Difference Formulas — Quick Reference}},
  fonttitle=\small\bfseries]
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.7}
\begin{tabular}{@{}l l@{}}
\textbf{Function} & \textbf{Forward Difference} \\
\hline
$x^{\underline{k}}$ (falling factorial) & $k\,x^{\underline{k-1}}$ \\
$(1+a)^n$ (discrete exponential) & $a\,(1+a)^n$ \\
$\binom{x}{k} = x^{\underline{k}}/k!$ & $\binom{x}{k-1}$ \\
$c$ (constant) & $0$ \\
$n$ & $1$ \\
$n^{\underline{2}} = n(n-1)$ & $2n$ \\
\end{tabular}
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{remark*}[Binomial coefficient as a special case]
The formula \(\Delta\binom{x}{k} = \binom{x}{k-1}\) follows immediately
from the falling factorial rule \(\Delta\,x^{\underline{k}} = k\,x^{\underline{k-1}}\)
by dividing through by \(k!\). It says that the binomial coefficient
\(\binom{x}{k}\) is its own natural ``unit'' in discrete calculus, analogous
to the monomial \(x^k/k!\) in the Taylor expansion.
\end{remark*}

\section*{Antiderivative Table}

By reversing the difference formulas, we obtain the corresponding
discrete antiderivatives:

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=gray!6, colframe=gray!40, arc=2pt,
  left=6pt, right=6pt, top=4pt, bottom=4pt,
  title={\small\textbf{Discrete Antiderivatives — Quick Reference}},
  fonttitle=\small\bfseries]
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.7}
\begin{tabular}{@{}l l@{}}
\textbf{Function \(f(n)\)} & \textbf{Antiderivative \(F(n)\) with \(\Delta F = f\)} \\
\hline
$x^{\underline{k}}$ & $\dfrac{x^{\underline{k+1}}}{k+1}$ \\[6pt]
$(1+a)^n\;(a\ne 0)$ & $\dfrac{(1+a)^n}{a}$ \\[6pt]
$\binom{x}{k}$ & $\binom{x}{k+1}$ \\[6pt]
$c$ (constant) & $cn$ \\
\end{tabular}
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{remark*}[Using the Fundamental Theorem]
Each antiderivative in the table, combined with the Fundamental Theorem
of Finite Calculus (Theorem~\ref{thm:ftfc}), yields an immediate
summation formula. For instance, from \(\Delta\bigl(\tfrac{x^{\underline{k+1}}}{k+1}\bigr) = x^{\underline{k}}\):
\[
\sum_{j=0}^{n-1} j^{\underline{k}}
= \frac{n^{\underline{k+1}}}{k+1}.
\]
This is the discrete analogue of \(\int_0^n x^k\,dx = \tfrac{n^{k+1}}{k+1}\).
\end{remark*}
