\begin{theorem*}[Negation Exchanges Supremum and Infimum]
Let $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ be a non-empty set. If $A$ is bounded below, then $\sup(-A) = -\inf A$.
\end{theorem*}

\begin{proof}[Professional Standard Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Let \(i=\inf A\). We prove that \(-i\) is the supremum of \(-A\).

First let \(y\in -A\). Then \(y=-x\) for some \(x\in A\). Since \(i\) is a
lower bound of \(A\), we have \(i\le x\). Multiplying by \(-1\) reverses the
inequality, so
\[
y=-x\le -i.
\]
Thus \(-i\) is an upper bound of \(-A\). In particular, \(-A\) is bounded
above.

Now let \(u\) be any upper bound of \(-A\). For every \(x\in A\), the element
\(-x\) belongs to \(-A\), so
\[
-x\le u.
\]
Multiplying by \(-1\) gives
\[
-u\le x
\]
for every \(x\in A\). Hence \(-u\) is a lower bound of \(A\). Since \(i=\inf A\)
is the greatest lower bound of \(A\), we have \(-u\le i\). Multiplying by
\(-1\) again gives
\[
-i\le u.
\]
Therefore every upper bound \(u\) of \(-A\) lies above \(-i\). Since \(-i\) is
itself an upper bound of \(-A\), it is the least upper bound of \(-A\). Hence
\[
\sup(-A)=-i=-\inf A.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Detailed Learning Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Set
\[
i=\inf A.
\]
We want to show that \(-i\) is the least upper bound of the reflected set
\[
-A=\{-a:a\in A\}.
\]

First we show that \(-i\) is an upper bound of \(-A\). Take an arbitrary
element \(y\in -A\). By the definition of \(-A\), there is some \(x\in A\)
such that
\[
y=-x.
\]
Because \(i=\inf A\), the number \(i\) is a lower bound of \(A\). Thus
\[
i\le x.
\]
After multiplying by \(-1\), the inequality reverses:
\[
-x\le -i.
\]
Since \(y=-x\), this gives \(y\le -i\). Therefore every element of \(-A\) is
less than or equal to \(-i\), so \(-i\) is an upper bound of \(-A\). This also
shows that \(-A\) is bounded above.

It remains to show that \(-i\) is the least upper bound. Let \(u\) be any
upper bound of \(-A\). Then every element of \(-A\) is less than or equal to
\(u\). In particular, for each \(x\in A\), the element \(-x\in -A\), so
\[
-x\le u.
\]
Multiplying by \(-1\) reverses the inequality:
\[
-u\le x.
\]
This holds for every \(x\in A\), so \(-u\) is a lower bound of \(A\). Since
\(i=\inf A\) is the greatest lower bound of \(A\), every lower bound of \(A\)
is less than or equal to \(i\). Hence
\[
-u\le i.
\]
Multiplying by \(-1\) one more time gives
\[
-i\le u.
\]
Thus every upper bound \(u\) of \(-A\) lies above \(-i\).

We have shown that \(-i\) is an upper bound of \(-A\) and that every upper
bound of \(-A\) is at least \(-i\). By the definition of supremum,
\[
\sup(-A)=-i=-\inf A.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark*}[Strategy: The Choice of Leastness Method]
In this proof, we establish the "leastness" property by comparing $-i$ against an arbitrary upper bound $u$ (the "Side A" method) rather than using the $\varepsilon$-characterization ("Side B"). While the $\varepsilon$-characterization is mathematically equivalent, it often requires "double negation" logic here---showing that $a < i + \varepsilon$ implies $-a > -i - \varepsilon$. When a proof already involves a set transformation like negation, it is often procedurally cleaner to use the direct definition of the supremum to avoid the mental overhead of tracking flipped inequalities across a tolerance variable.
\end{remark*}

\begin{remark*}[Proof structure]
Negation reverses order. Lower bounds of \(A\) become upper bounds of \(-A\),
and the greatest lower bound becomes the least upper bound after multiplying
all inequalities by \(-1\).
\end{remark*}
