\begin{proposition*}[Maximum Implies Supremum]
If a set $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ has a maximum $m$, then $m$ is also the supremum of $A$.
\end{proposition*}

\begin{proof}[Professional Standard Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Suppose \(m\) is a maximum of \(A\). Then \(m\in A\) and
\[
x\le m
\]
for every \(x\in A\). Hence \(m\) is an upper bound of \(A\).

Now let \(u\) be any upper bound of \(A\). Since \(m\in A\), the upper-bound
property of \(u\) gives \(m\le u\). Thus every upper bound \(u\) of \(A\)
satisfies \(m\le u\). Therefore \(m\) is the least upper bound of \(A\), so
\[
m=\sup A.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Detailed Learning Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
We prove that \(m\) satisfies the two defining properties of the supremum of
\(A\).

First, since \(m\) is a maximum of \(A\), the definition of maximum gives
\[
m\in A
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
x\le m \quad\text{for every }x\in A.
\]
The second condition says exactly that \(m\) is an upper bound of \(A\).

Second, we show that \(m\) is the least upper bound. Let \(u\) be an arbitrary
upper bound of \(A\). Because \(u\) is an upper bound, every element of \(A\)
is less than or equal to \(u\). Since \(m\in A\), we may apply this to the
element \(m\) itself and obtain
\[
m\le u.
\]
Thus every upper bound \(u\) of \(A\) lies above \(m\).

So \(m\) is an upper bound of \(A\), and every upper bound of \(A\) is at least
\(m\). By the definition of supremum,
\[
m=\sup A.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark*}[Proof structure]
A maximum is already an upper bound because it dominates every element of the
set. It is least among upper bounds because it is itself an element of the set:
any upper bound must bound \(m\) too.
\end{remark*}
