\begin{theorem*}[Infimum-Supremum Comparison]
For any non-empty bounded set $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, the infimum of $A$ is less than or equal to the supremum of $A$.
\end{theorem*}

\begin{proof}[Professional Standard Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Because \(A\) is nonempty, choose \(x\in A\). Since \(\inf A\) is a lower
bound of \(A\), we have
\[
\inf A\le x.
\]
Since \(\sup A\) is an upper bound of \(A\), we have
\[
x\le \sup A.
\]
By transitivity of the order on \(\mathbb{R}\),
\[
\inf A\le \sup A.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Detailed Learning Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
The set \(A\) is nonempty, so there exists an element \(x\in A\).

By definition, \(\inf A\) is the greatest lower bound of \(A\). In
particular, it is a lower bound of \(A\). Thus every element of \(A\) lies
above \(\inf A\), so for our chosen \(x\in A\),
\[
\inf A\le x.
\]

Similarly, \(\sup A\) is the least upper bound of \(A\). In particular, it is
an upper bound of \(A\). Thus every element of \(A\) lies below \(\sup A\), so
for the same \(x\in A\),
\[
x\le \sup A.
\]

Combining the two inequalities gives
\[
\inf A\le x\le \sup A.
\]
Therefore
\[
\inf A\le \sup A.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark*}[Proof structure]
Choose one element of the nonempty set. The lower-bound part of the infimum
definition places \(\inf A\) below that element, and the upper-bound part of
the supremum definition places the element below \(\sup A\). Transitivity
finishes the comparison.
\end{remark*}
