\begin{corollary*}[Bounded Second Derivative Implies \(C^{1,1}\)]
Let \(I\subseteq\mathbb{R}\) be an interval and let \(f\in C^2(I)\). If
\(|f''(x)|\leq M\) for all \(x\in I\), then \(f\in C^{1,1}(I)\), and \(f'\)
is Lipschitz with constant \(M\).
\end{corollary*}

\begin{proof}[Professional Standard Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
Since \(f\in C^2(I)\), the derivative \(f'\) is differentiable on \(I\) and
\((f')'=f''\). The hypothesis gives \(|(f')'(x)|\leq M\) for every
\(x\in I\). Applying the derivative-bound-implies-Lipschitz corollary to
the function \(f'\) yields
\[
  |f'(x)-f'(y)|\leq M|x-y|
  \qquad\text{for all }x,y\in I.
\]
Thus \(f'\) is Lipschitz with constant \(M\). Because \(f\in C^2(I)\) also
implies \(f\in C^1(I)\), the definition gives \(f\in C^{1,1}(I)\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Detailed Learning Proof]
\LRAProofBodyStart
The derivative-bound corollary turns a pointwise slope bound into a global
Lipschitz estimate. Here the function whose slope is being bounded is not
\(f\), but \(f'\). Its derivative is \(f''\). If \(|f''|\leq M\), then the
slope of \(f'\) is bounded by \(M\), so \(f'\) changes by at most
\(M|x-y|\) between any two points \(x,y\in I\). That is exactly the
Lipschitz condition required in the definition of \(C^{1,1}\).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark*}[Proof structure]
Apply the derivative-bound Lipschitz theorem one derivative level higher.
\end{remark*}
